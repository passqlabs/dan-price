how i think

AI in business has two stages. Most people are stuck on the first.

How I think AI actually changes a company, and why mine runs on a folder of text files.

AI lands in a business in two stages, and they are not the same size. Stage one is adopting AI into the way you already work: faster drafts, quicker research, a copilot inside the tools you already have. Stage two is rebuilding the work itself around what AI has just made cheap. Almost everyone is parked in stage one. The work that interests me is stage two.

Stage one is worth doing, but it has a ceiling. AI can't fix a broken process. If your sales process closes 5% of leads, automating it doesn't get you to 20%, it gets you a 5% close rate with less typing. Done properly, stage one isn't "add AI", it's fix the workflow first, then add AI to what's left. Most "AI transformation" stops at the less-typing version and calls it a day.

Stage two asks a different question. Not "where can AI help the way we work now?" but "what would we build if a team of capable agents was basically free?" Answer it honestly and you stop tuning the old model and start designing a new one.

At PassQ I stopped asking where AI could slot into our existing operating model and started rebuilding the model around agents, review gates and plain-text state. The result isn't a chatbot bolted on the side. It's the working surface the company runs from, and it's a folder of markdown files. The agents read and write plain text; every output is a file I sign off before it ships; version control is the audit trail. Three things follow from that one boring choice: any model can read a folder, so I'm not locked to a vendor; anyone can open a file and read the real state of the business in plain English; and the human stays in the loop by default.

founder-os/
├─ STATUS.md            live state, read at the start of every session
├─ _strategy/           the thesis, the milestones, the bets
├─ team/                the agent roster
├─ pipelines/
│  ├─ sales/            prospect → outreach → proposal
│  ├─ marketing/        SEO · social · email
│  ├─ investment/       the raise
│  └─ product/          spec → build → review
├─ owners-inbox/        agent output, waiting on my sign-off
└─ _integrations/       MCP: Gmail · Calendar · Drive · CRM

Founder OS, top level. No app, no database. The company's state is a folder you can open and read.

I didn't invent this shape. Jake Van Clief's Interpretable Context Methodology makes the case for folders over orchestration frameworks, and it's where the idea clicked for me. What I did was bet a real company on it, in production, and watch it hold.

And it isn't only this company. When my last business wound down, the agency that came next needed to run without a back office. I built the system it runs on, and the same reps now place several times what they used to, because the software absorbs the compliance, payroll and admin they used to need people for. Same humans, different structure. That's stage two.

I'm not a designer or an engineer by training. What I bring is twenty years next to brilliant ones, doing presales for social technology and building teams around tools that didn't exist the year before. That is where you learn what matters most in stage two: knowing what is worth building, and what "good" means for the person who has to use it. AI will build almost anything you can describe. Describing the right thing, and knowing it when you see it, is the work now.

I'm not theorising from a whiteboard. I've built and run businesses, and technology only counts if it does something real on a Tuesday morning. The two-stage view isn't a slide. It's how I decide what to build next.

Stage one makes you faster. Stage two makes you a different company. I'd rather build the different company.

Source: Jake Van Clief, "Stop Building AI Agents. Use This Folder System Instead". The video that changed how I thought about working with AI.
